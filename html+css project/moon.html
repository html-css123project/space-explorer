Discovering the Moons of the Solar System

Moons are the silent companions of planets — rocky, icy, and mysterious worlds that orbit their parent planets. From our own Moon to the volcanic Io and the oceanic Europa, moons have stories waiting to be uncovered.

What Are Moons?

Moons (also called natural satellites) are celestial bodies that orbit planets and dwarf planets. Our solar system is home to over 200 known moons, each with its own geology, atmosphere, and history.

Famous Moons of Our Solar System

Moon Name Planet Highlights
Moon (Luna) Earth Only natural satellite of Earth; affects tides; first to be visited by humans.
Europa Jupiter Believed to have a subsurface ocean; strong candidate for alien life.
Io Jupiter Most volcanically active object in the solar system.
Titan Saturn Thick atmosphere and liquid methane lakes; bigger than Mercury.
Ganymede Jupiter Largest moon in the solar system; has its own magnetic field.
Triton Neptune Retrograde orbit; may be a captured dwarf planet.
Phobos & Deimos Mars Small, irregularly shaped moons likely captured asteroids.

Moon Facts – Quick Cosmic Insights

  • Earth’s Moon: Only celestial body visited by humans (Apollo missions).
  • Volcanic Activity: Io has over 400 active volcanoes.
  • Subsurface Oceans: Europa and Enceladus may have liquid water beneath ice.
  • Atmosphere: Titan has a thick nitrogen-rich atmosphere.
  • Double Moons: Some asteroids and dwarf planets have tiny moons.
  • Capture: Some moons were captured from asteroid belts or the Kuiper Belt.

Why Study Moons?

Moons help scientists understand planetary formation, geology, and even the potential for life beyond Earth. Future missions to Europa, Titan, and our own Moon aim to uncover secrets beneath their surfaces.
