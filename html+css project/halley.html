Halley’s Comet – The Comet That Keeps Coming Back

A celestial traveler known since ancient times, Halley’s Comet orbits the Sun and returns every 75–76 years.

Quick Facts About Halley’s Comet

  • Type: Periodic Comet
  • Named After: Edmond Halley (British astronomer)
  • Orbital Period: ~75–76 years
  • Next Appearance: 2061
  • Last Seen: 1986
  • Origin: Kuiper Belt region

What Makes Halley’s Comet Special?

Halley’s Comet is the **only known short-period comet** that is regularly visible to the naked eye and **returns within a human lifetime**. Its appearances have been documented for over 2,000 years, including sightings in ancient Babylon, China, and medieval Europe.

Edmond Halley correctly predicted its return in 1758 using Newton’s laws, proving comets orbit the Sun and marking a turning point in astronomy.

Orbit and Composition

Halley’s orbit is **elongated and retrograde** — it travels around the Sun opposite to the planets. The nucleus is a dark, potato-shaped object about **15 kilometers long** and made of **ice, dust, and rocky material** — a classic “dirty snowball.”

As it nears the Sun, it develops a glowing **coma** and long **ion and dust tails** that can stretch millions of kilometers into space.

Famous Appearances and Cultural Impact

  • Appeared in the **Bayeux Tapestry** (1066 AD) during the Norman conquest of England.
  • Last observed in **1986**, visited by multiple spacecraft like **Giotto** (ESA).
  • Linked with historical events such as the death of **Mark Twain**, who was born and died in Halley’s Comet years (1835–1910).

What’s Next?

Halley’s Comet will return in **2061**, appearing brighter than in 1986 and likely visible to the naked eye once again. It is a reminder of the predictable and powerful rhythms of our solar system.
