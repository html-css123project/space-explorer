Moon (Luna) – Earth's Loyal Companion

The Moon, also called Luna, is Earth's only natural satellite. It has fascinated humanity for millennia and plays a vital role in our planet’s tides, rotation, and cultural imagination.

Overview of the Moon

The Moon is the fifth-largest natural satellite in the Solar System and orbits Earth at an average distance of about 384,400 kilometers (238,855 miles). It completes a full orbit roughly every 27.3 days and shows phases as it reflects sunlight.

Key Features

  • Diameter: 3,474 km
  • Gravity: About 1/6th that of Earth
  • Surface: Rocky, dusty with craters, mountains, and lava plains (maria)
  • Atmosphere: Extremely thin exosphere (not breathable)
  • Temperatures: Ranges from -173°C at night to +127°C in sunlight

Origin and Formation

The Moon is believed to have formed about 4.5 billion years ago after a Mars-sized object (called Theia) collided with the early Earth. Debris from the impact eventually coalesced into the Moon.

Moon Phases

The Moon goes through eight major phases every 29.5 days:

  1. New Moon
  2. Waxing Crescent
  3. First Quarter
  4. Waxing Gibbous
  5. Full Moon
  6. Waning Gibbous
  7. Last Quarter
  8. Waning Crescent

Moon and Earth

The Moon stabilizes Earth’s axial tilt, helps regulate its rotation, and causes ocean tides through gravitational pull. Over time, it's slowly drifting away from Earth — by about 3.8 cm per year.

Exploration and Missions

The Moon was the first celestial body visited by spacecraft. NASA’s Apollo 11 mission in 1969 made history with Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin becoming the first humans to walk on the Moon. Since then, many missions have explored its surface and orbit, with renewed interest from countries like China, India, and the U.S.

Interesting Moon Facts

  • The same side of the Moon always faces Earth — it’s tidally locked.
  • The Moon has moonquakes, caused by tidal stress from Earth.
  • Its surface is covered in a layer of fine dust called regolith.
  • You'd weigh only about 16.5% of your Earth weight on the Moon.
  • There's water ice in permanently shadowed craters at the lunar poles.
