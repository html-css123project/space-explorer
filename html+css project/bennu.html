Bennu – A Primitive Asteroid With Ancient Secrets

Bennu is a small, carbon-rich asteroid that holds clues to the early solar system and possibly to the origins of life.

Quick Facts About Bennu

  • Type: Near-Earth Asteroid (Apollo group)
  • Discovered: September 11, 1999
  • Size: ~492 meters in diameter
  • Orbital Period: ~1.2 Earth years
  • Closest Approach: Every 6 years (approx.)

Why Is Bennu Important?

Bennu is considered a **carbonaceous asteroid**, meaning it contains **organic molecules** and **water-bearing minerals** — the essential ingredients for life. Because of its primitive composition, Bennu offers scientists a time capsule from the early solar system, over **4.5 billion years old**.

OSIRIS-REx Sample Return Mission

In 2020, NASA’s **OSIRIS-REx spacecraft** successfully collected a sample from Bennu’s surface — a historic milestone. The return capsule landed back on Earth in September 2023 with precious material that scientists are now analyzing.

This mission will help us understand how planets formed and how organic compounds may have been delivered to Earth.

Surface and Shape

  • Bennu has a **spinning top shape**, with a bulging equator caused by its fast rotation.
  • Its surface is rocky and covered in loose **rubble** — not smooth or sandy.
  • Bennu has small **active plumes** that occasionally eject particles into space.

Potential Earth Impact?

Bennu’s orbit brings it close to Earth. While it is **not currently a threat**, scientists are monitoring it carefully. There’s a **1 in 1,750 chance** it could impact Earth between now and the year 2300 — a low probability, but worth studying.

Understanding Bennu’s **trajectory** and internal structure improves planetary defense strategies.

Interesting Facts

  • Named after an Egyptian bird symbolizing rebirth.
  • Spins once every 4.3 hours.
  • Delivers data on solar radiation effects (Yarkovsky effect).
  • Its sample is the largest brought back from space since the Apollo Moon missions.
