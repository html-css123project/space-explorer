Venus – Earth's Mysterious Twin

Venus is the second planet from the Sun and often called Earth's sister due to its similar size and structure. But beneath its clouds lies a scorching, toxic world unlike any other.

Quick Introduction to Venus

Venus is the hottest planet in the solar system, with surface temperatures hot enough to melt lead. Despite being similar in size to Earth, Venus spins slowly and in the opposite direction, and its thick clouds trap heat in a runaway greenhouse effect.

Key Features of Venus

  • Distance from Sun: About 108 million km (67 million miles).
  • Surface: Rocky and volcanic, covered by thick clouds of sulfuric acid.
  • Atmosphere: 96% carbon dioxide, extremely dense.
  • Temperature: Around 465°C (870°F) — hotter than Mercury!
  • Moons: None.
  • Rotation: Spins backward (retrograde) — a day lasts 243 Earth days.

Formation and Composition

Venus formed from the solar nebula about 4.6 billion years ago and is made mostly of rock and metal. Its internal structure is similar to Earth's, with a crust, mantle, and large iron core. However, its surface is shaped largely by volcanic activity rather than plate tectonics.

Surface and Atmosphere

Venus is a planet of extremes. Its surface pressure is over 90 times that of Earth’s — equivalent to being 900 meters under water. The thick atmosphere is filled with clouds of sulfuric acid, and there are no seasons due to its minimal tilt. Most of its surface is flat plains, with large volcanoes, highland regions, and massive lava flows.

Exploration Missions

  • Venera Missions (Soviet Union): First to land on Venus. Venera 7 was the first spacecraft to send data from its surface (1970).
  • Magellan (NASA, 1990s): Mapped 98% of Venus’ surface using radar imaging.
  • Future Missions: ESA's EnVision and NASA’s VERITAS & DAVINCI+ are planned to study Venus in the 2030s.

Fun Facts About Venus

  1. Venus spins in the opposite direction of most planets (retrograde rotation).
  2. A day on Venus is longer than its year!
  3. It has no moons or rings — just like Mercury.
  4. Venus is often the third-brightest object in the sky (after the Sun and Moon).
  5. Venus shows phases, just like the Moon, when viewed from Earth.
  6. Its thick clouds reflect 70% of the sunlight, making it incredibly bright.
