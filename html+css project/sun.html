The Sun – The Life-Giving Star

The Sun is a giant ball of burning hydrogen and helium at the center of our Solar System. It provides heat, light, and energy to all life on Earth and governs the orbits of planets.

Quick Facts About the Sun

  • Type: G-type main-sequence star (Yellow Dwarf)
  • Diameter: 1.39 million km
  • Mass: 99.86% of total Solar System mass
  • Temperature: Surface: ~5,500°C | Core: ~15 million °C
  • Distance from Earth: ~150 million km (1 AU)

Structure of the Sun

  • Core: Where nuclear fusion happens (hydrogen into helium)
  • Radiative Zone: Energy moves slowly outward via radiation
  • Convective Zone: Hot gases circulate in convection currents
  • Photosphere: The visible “surface” of the Sun
  • Chromosphere: Thin layer above the photosphere
  • Corona: The outermost and hottest part of the Sun's atmosphere, visible during solar eclipses

Sun's Role in the Solar System

The Sun's gravity holds all the planets, moons, comets, and asteroids in orbit. Its energy drives Earth’s weather, ocean currents, and enables life through photosynthesis.

Without the Sun, the Solar System would collapse into darkness and cold. Learn more about the Solar System here.

Solar Activity

  • Sunspots: Cooler, darker areas on the surface caused by magnetic activity
  • Solar Flares: Sudden explosions of energy from the surface
  • Coronal Mass Ejections (CMEs): Huge eruptions of solar plasma that can affect Earth’s magnetic field and satellites

Solar Cycles

The Sun goes through an **11-year cycle** of solar activity, from solar minimum (quiet) to solar maximum (more sunspots and flares). These cycles affect space weather and can disrupt Earth’s communication systems.

Fun Facts About the Sun

  • About 1 million Earths could fit inside the Sun.
  • It takes sunlight about 8 minutes to reach Earth.
  • The Sun is middle-aged (about 4.6 billion years old) and will live for another 5 billion years.
  • Eventually, the Sun will become a red giant, then a white dwarf.
