Polaris – The Guiding Star of the North

Polaris, the North Star, has been a celestial compass for centuries. Nearly aligned with Earth's north celestial pole, it remains fixed in the night sky.

Quick Facts About Polaris

  • Type: Yellow supergiant (F7)
  • Constellation: Ursa Minor
  • Distance from Earth: ~433 light-years
  • Other Names: Alpha Ursae Minoris
  • Apparent Magnitude: ~1.98

Why Polaris Is the North Star

Polaris is almost directly above Earth's **North Pole**, meaning it stays fixed in the same spot in the night sky while other stars appear to rotate around it. This makes it invaluable for **navigation** and orientation.

A Multi-Star System

Polaris is not just a single star — it’s a **triple star system**:

  • Polaris A: A yellow supergiant and the main star we see.
  • Polaris Ab: A close dwarf companion to Polaris A.
  • Polaris B: A smaller star orbiting far from the main pair.

Variable Brightness

Polaris is also a **Cepheid variable star**, meaning its brightness changes over time in a regular pattern. These types of stars are used to **measure cosmic distances**.

Changing Role Over Time

Due to Earth's **axial precession**, Polaris wasn’t always the North Star — and it won’t always be. In 12,000 years, **Vega** will take its place. Thousands of years ago, **Thuban** served as the North Star.

Fun Facts About Polaris

  • Used by sailors, explorers, and travelers for centuries to find true north.
  • Its brightness and position made it sacred in many ancient cultures.
  • Despite being famous, it’s only about the 50th brightest star in the sky.
  • Its light takes over 400 years to reach us.
